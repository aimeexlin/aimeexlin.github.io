BIOMECHANICAL AND ERGONOMIC ANALYSIS OF JOINT MOBILISATION TASKS IN PHYSICAL THERAPY

This project was completed for EXERSCI 309, a practicum project in exercise science taken as an elective. I was supervised by Dr Yanxin Zhang from the Department of Exercise Science. I investigated whether performing joint mobilisation tasks such as shoulder, hip, and pelvic joint mobilisation increase the risk for work-related musculoskeletal disorders (WMSDs) in physical therapists using motion capture data collected from a study conducted in China. Although I didn’t collect the data used in the project, prior to Covid-19 lockdown during the semester I learnt how to use the Azure Kinect system to capture motion data in the biomechanics lab. I carried out biomechanical and ergonomic analyses using an existing software to find RULA and REBA angles, low back compression forces, and strength capabilities in the joints at the most awkward posture for each task for every subject. For each dependent variable measured, a two-way mixed ANOVA was performed using R. The results showed that all three joint mobilisation tasks investigated can increase the risk of WMSDs for both female and male physical therapists.

REPRESENTING A BIOLOGICAL SYSTEM

This project was completed for BIOMENG 341, a third-year bioinstrumentation and design course. I worked with four other third-year biomedical engineering students to design and create a system that replicates the distension of the frog vocal sac. We modelled the system using a balloon inflated and deflated through a syringe and investigated the relationships between pressure, temperature, and volume, as well as the flow impedance and stress-strain relationships. Due to Covid-19 lockdown during the semester, the project was split into subsystems so the work could be more fairly divided between the group members. I contributed to the volume measurement by analysing a video recording of the balloon being inflated and deflated. The video was recorded by another group member who had set up the experiment and also recorded pressure and temperature data simultaneously. The analysis was done in LabVIEW and exported the measurements so all the data generated thus far could be used by other group members to analyse the flow impedance, stress, and strain. We were able to successfully measure everything required for the project.

FRAMEWORK TO CAPTURE AND ANALYSE DEXTEROUS OBJECT MANIPULATION

This project was completed for a Summer Research Scholarship at the Auckland Bioengineering Institute (ABI). I was supervised by Professor Leo Cheng, Dr Nira Paskaranandavadivel, and Jaime Lara. I constructed two sets of standard manipulation objects capable of encasing a Shimmer3 wireless multi-sensor unit capable of streaming 9 degrees of freedom inertial sensing information. One set was constructed using Ecoflex 00-30 silicone and the other using 3D printed PLA plastic. I also extracted the Shimmer3 data to visualise the movement of the standard manipulation objects and was able to estimate object orientations in near real-time. Positions were unable to be estimated accurately due to bias drift.

HANDHELD COLOUR METER: THE COLOURSTICK

This project was completed for BIOMENG 241, a second-year bioinstrumentation and design course. I worked with three other second-year biomedical engineering students to design the case, circuitry, and software to build a handheld skin colour measurement device. The device operated by measuring the reflectances of red, green, and blue LEDs using a photodiode. I contributed to the design and testing of the circuitry and software, which involved design and programming on Altium Designer and LabVIEW, and testing with a breadboard and the assembled device.

OPERATION VACCINATION

This project was completed for ENGSCI 233, a second-year computational techniques and computer systems course. We were given the hypothetical scenario of delivering Covid-19 vaccines from the airport to all the rest homes in Auckland by four couriers travelling only using Auckland’s public transportation system. I developed an algorithm using Python that split the rest homes into four groups based on geographic location and used a network to return the shortest distance path and time taken for each group.

COMBINING IMAGES

This project was completed for ENGGEN 131, a first-year engineering computation and software development course. I wrote MATLAB code that creates two different kinds of images from an arbitrary sequence of images that feature someone (or something) moving through a static scene. These images could be from a series of photos or a selection of frames taken from a movie. One kind of image was an action shot, a single image that features the moving person or object shown in different locations. The other kind of image was one where the action has been removed, eliminating the moving person (or object) from the image, leaving only the static scene visible.

MITIGATING POWER-LINE CAUSED FIRES WITH DATA ANALYSIS AND CITIZEN SCIENCE

This project was completed for the New York Academy of Sciences’ Junior Academy Wildfires: Tracking, Prevention, and Containment Challenge. I worked with five other high school students from India, Ukraine, and the US to design a solution to mitigate power-line caused fires. Our solution was a predictive analytics software that uses data from weather stations, government data on historical and ongoing fires fires, and citizen reports on fallen power lines and dry vegetation from an app to identify high-risk areas and suggest management plans for these areas for local governments and communities. I contributed to the background research by creating and sending out a survey to residents of California, and the design of the predictive analytics software user interface. We were finalists in the challenge and presented our project to 100+ attendees at the 2019 Global STEM Alliance Summit in New York City. We were also featured in the Scientific American.
